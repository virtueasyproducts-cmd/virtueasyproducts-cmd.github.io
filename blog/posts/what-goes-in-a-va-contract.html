What Goes in a VA Contract (and What Happens Without One)

Virtueasy · Contracts & Scope · 8 min read

You hired a virtual assistant. Things started well. Then scope crept, an invoice went unpaid, or you discovered your VA was using the work they built for you in their own portfolio without asking. Now you are in an awkward conversation with no paperwork to back you up.

This happens more than it should, and almost all of it is preventable.

A contract is not about distrust. It is about clarity. It sets the terms before anyone is annoyed, before money is on the line, and before something ambiguous becomes a real problem. Whether you are a business owner hiring your first VA or a VA building out your client process, a written agreement is the minimum standard for a professional arrangement.

Here is what a solid VA contract actually covers, and a quick look at what skipping each part tends to look like in practice.

Scope of Work

This section defines exactly what the VA is doing, how often, and at what level of detail. It should name specific tasks, not vague categories. "Social media support" is not a scope of work. "Scheduling and publishing three Instagram posts per week using approved captions" is.

Without it: A client expects daily email management. The VA understood it as responding to flagged emails twice a week. Neither is wrong based on what was agreed verbally. Both are frustrated three weeks in. If this sounds familiar, scope creep is usually where it ends up.

Payment Terms and Late Fees

This covers the rate, the billing cycle, the payment method, and what happens when an invoice is overdue. It should specify when invoices are sent, when they are due, and what the late fee structure looks like. Some VAs charge a percentage after a set number of days. Others pause work. The method matters less than the fact that it is written down.

Without it: A client pays 45 days late with no acknowledgment that it is late. The VA has no documented terms to point to and no leverage beyond an uncomfortable message. The relationship gets awkward. The VA either absorbs it or loses the client by pushing back.

Revision Limits

If the VA is producing deliverables, such as written content, graphics, templates, or reports, the contract should specify how many rounds of revisions are included. Two rounds is common. Unlimited is not sustainable and invites scope creep disguised as feedback.

Without it: A client receives a batch of ten email drafts. They revise them. Then revise the revisions. Then ask for a different tone entirely and start again. The VA has spent three times the expected hours on one deliverable with no way to charge for the extra time, because nothing defined where the work ended.

Ownership of Work

This is the intellectual property clause, and it matters more than most people realize. Who owns the content, strategy, templates, or systems the VA creates? In most cases, if the client is paying for it, they want to own it. But that transfer of ownership needs to be stated explicitly. Some VAs also retain the right to show work in their portfolio, which is reasonable, but that too should be agreed upon in writing.

Without it: A VA builds a full content system for a client, and the client later discovers the same templates, lightly modified, being used in another client's business. Or a VA shares client materials in a public portfolio without permission, including confidential information. Both situations create real problems that a clear IP clause would have prevented.

Termination Notice

Either side should be able to end the arrangement, but not without warning. A standard notice period, often two weeks, gives both parties time to wrap up work, transfer files, and avoid abrupt disruption. The clause should also specify what happens to any work in progress and whether a final invoice is owed at termination.

Without it: A client decides to end the arrangement on a Friday afternoon and stops responding. The VA has work mid-completion and an outstanding invoice with no clear recourse. Or a VA disappears mid-project for a new client, leaving the business owner scrambling with no transition plan.

Confidentiality

A VA often has access to email inboxes, financial data, customer lists, internal processes, passwords, and business strategy. A confidentiality clause, sometimes called an NDA or non-disclosure provision, establishes that this information stays private during and after the working relationship.

Without it: A VA who leaves on bad terms has no legal obligation not to discuss what they saw inside your business. That might not matter in most cases. In some, it does. Confidential client data, unreleased products, pricing structures, internal conflicts, any of it could surface without a written commitment to keep it private.

Communication and Availability

This one is often skipped but worth including. The contract should specify how the VA and client communicate, which platform they use, what the expected response time is, and what hours the VA is available. This prevents the assumption mismatch that happens when one party expects same-day responses and the other is working across time zones with a different workload.

Without it: A client sends urgent tasks on a Sunday expecting a Monday morning turnaround and discovers their VA does not check messages on weekends. Neither is wrong. But without written expectations, it reads like negligence. Setting this out is part of a strong onboarding process.

Dispute Resolution

If something goes wrong and both sides disagree, how is it handled? The contract should name a process, even a simple one. Many freelance agreements specify that disputes are handled through mediation before any legal action. It also helps to name the governing jurisdiction so there is no argument about which state or country's rules apply.

Without it: A disagreement about unpaid work becomes a standoff. Neither side knows what the other can actually do. Threats get made. The relationship ends badly and nothing is resolved cleanly.

Getting the Contract Right Before Work Starts

None of this is about being difficult or assuming the worst. It is about having a shared document that both sides can return to when memory gets fuzzy or expectations drift. A contract does not prevent every problem. It prevents the problems that come from ambiguity.

If you are a VA building your client process, the contract belongs in your onboarding sequence, sent alongside or immediately after your proposal. If you are a business owner hiring a VA, asking for a written agreement is not a red flag to raise. It is a green one. It signals that the person you are working with takes the arrangement seriously.

A one-page agreement that covers scope, payment, revisions, IP, confidentiality, termination, and communication is not overkill. It is the baseline.

This post is not legal advice. If your arrangement involves significant money, sensitive data, or complex deliverables, a lawyer familiar with freelance or contractor agreements is worth consulting.
