How to Ask VA Clients for a Testimonial (With Templates)

Virtueasy | 6 min read

Your happy clients won't write you a testimonial unless you ask. Here is exactly when to ask, how to make it easy, and the words to use.

You finished the project. The client is happy. They said something along the lines of "this has been so helpful, I don't know what I'd do without you." And then... nothing. No testimonial. No review. No social proof you can actually use.

That's not because they don't want to help you. It's because asking for a testimonial feels awkward, and most people won't do it without a nudge, a prompt, and a clear path to follow.

This post walks you through the when, the how, and the exact words to use, so you can turn genuine client satisfaction into credibility that works for you when you're not in the room.

Why Freelance Client Testimonials Actually Matter

Before the tactics, let's be clear about what testimonials do.

When a potential client lands on your website or receives your proposal, they're trying to assess risk. Can this person actually deliver? Will they be reliable? Is the price worth it?

You can write the best bio in the world and it won't carry the weight of a single sentence from a real client who says "she saved me 10 hours a week within the first month."

Testimonials reduce perceived risk. They shift the conversation from "will this work?" to "how do I get started?" That's the difference between someone closing your tab and someone sending an enquiry.

For VAs specifically, this matters even more because the services you offer, inbox management, scheduling, research, admin, are hard to show in a portfolio the way a designer or photographer can. A testimonial becomes one of the clearest signals of your competence.

When to Ask: Timing Your VA Testimonial Request

Timing is the biggest variable most VAs get wrong. They either wait too long, until the relationship has cooled, or they ask at a moment when the client hasn't felt the full value yet.

Here are the moments that work best:

  • Right after a clear win. You delivered a project on time, sorted out a process that was causing the client stress, or cleared a backlog. The result is visible and the client is genuinely relieved or impressed. That's the window.
  • At the end of a project or contract. Natural closure creates a natural moment to reflect. It doesn't feel like an imposition.
  • After a positive comment. If a client says something like "this week was so much smoother with you handling that," reply with a thank you and then a direct ask. You're not fishing for a compliment, they already gave you one.
  • At a milestone in an ongoing retainer. Three months in, six months in, when you've built enough history together that the impact is undeniable.

Avoid asking when a client is in the middle of a stressful period, when something has gone wrong and isn't resolved yet, or right when you've had to push back on scope. Read the moment.

How to Make It Easy for Your Client

Most clients don't leave testimonials because they don't know what to write. They want to help you but they stare at a blank page and give up.

Your job is to remove that friction entirely.

The most effective way to do this is to give them a prompt or a short list of questions. You're not putting words in their mouth, you're giving them a starting point.

Try prompts like:

  • What were you struggling with before we worked together?
  • What's one specific thing I helped you with that made a real difference?
  • Would you recommend working with me, and if so, who do you think would benefit most?

You can include these in a message or create a short Google Form that takes two minutes to complete. The easier you make it, the more responses you'll get.

Copy-Paste Templates for Different Scenarios

These are starting points. Adjust the tone to match how you communicate with each client.

After project completion

"Hi [name], now that we've wrapped up [project], I wanted to say thank you for being such a great client to work with. If you'd be open to it, a short testimonial would mean a lot to me and genuinely helps other business owners feel confident reaching out. No pressure at all, but if you're happy to, even two or three sentences would be brilliant. To make it easy, here are a few prompts you could use: [insert prompts]. You can reply directly here or fill in this quick form: [link]. Thanks so much either way."

After a positive comment or message

"That's so kind of you to say, thank you! Would you be comfortable letting me use something like that as a testimonial? I can quote you directly, or if you'd prefer to write a line or two yourself, here are some prompts to help: [insert prompts]. Either works for me."

For ongoing retainer clients at a milestone

"Hi [name], it's been [timeframe] since we started working together and I've really enjoyed being part of your team. I'm currently updating my website and testimonials from clients I respect make a big difference. If you'd be happy to share a few words about our work together, here are some prompts to get you started: [insert prompts]. No rush and absolutely no obligation, but I'd be grateful if you felt it was warranted."

If you need to follow up

"Hi [name], just a gentle nudge on the testimonial I mentioned a couple of weeks ago. I know things get busy. If it's still something you're happy to do, the prompts are below. If now isn't a good time, no worries at all."

One follow up is appropriate. After that, let it go.

Where to Use Testimonials Once You Have Them

Getting the testimonial is only half the job. Here's where they do the most work:

  • Your website. A dedicated testimonials page is useful, but testimonials placed next to your services descriptions or on your homepage convert better because they're contextual.
  • Your proposals. Adding one or two short client quotes to a proposal, near your pricing or your list of deliverables, addresses objections at the exact moment a prospect is weighing their decision.
  • LinkedIn recommendations. Ask clients if they'd be willing to post the testimonial as a LinkedIn recommendation too. It's the same content but it adds a layer of verification because it's attached to a real profile.
  • Social media. A short quote with context makes solid content, especially if you frame it around the problem the client had, not just the compliment. "Before we worked together, my inbox was out of control. Now I actually get to focus on the work I love." That's a story.
  • Email signature or enquiry responses. A single line testimonial in your email signature adds quiet credibility in every communication.

A Note on Hesitant Clients

Some clients will be cautious about being quoted publicly, especially if they work in a regulated industry or a competitive niche. Respect that completely.

You can offer to use only their first name, their job title without the company name, or a general description like "founder of a product-based business in the UK." You can also ask if they'd prefer to write a LinkedIn recommendation instead of something you'd post publicly.

The goal is usable social proof, not a name to drop. Most of the time, even a partial attribution is enough.

Asking for testimonials is a professional skill, not a favour. You've done good work. Give the people who benefited from it a clear, easy way to say so.

Start with one client, someone who's recently expressed satisfaction, and use one of the templates above. See what comes back. Most people are more willing than you think.
